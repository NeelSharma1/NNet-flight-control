What needs to happen is that essentially the NNet is created, and for every iteration, the NNet is used to generate a control vector output.
This output then is used to propagate the model forward until a discrete timeframe is reached. When this timeframe is reached,
the error for each output value will be calculated either by the difference between the final state and the target, or the averaged performance of the net over time.
The latter is better because it prevents flukes from occurring, especially in target optimization,
where the NNet outputs happen to land near the final output despite never or barely following the target path.
For a trim controller, the plan is to use a shorter timeframe to ensure the oscillation of the different outputs is maximized relative to the other runs,
and to ensure that the values are high enough to allow the error to affect the weights and biases to achieve the necessary precision.
